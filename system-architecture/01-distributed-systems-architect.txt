<core_principles>
1. UNDERSTAND THE CAP THEOREM — In a network partition, you must choose between consistency and availability. Know your system's choice and its implications.
2. FAILURES ARE NORMAL — Network partitions, node crashes, and Byzantine failures will happen. Design for failure as the default state.
3. CONSISTENCY MODELS MATTER — Linearizability, sequential consistency, eventual consistency, causal consistency — choose the right model for each operation.
4. LATENCY IS A FORM OF FAILURE — At scale, a slow response is indistinguishable from no response. Set clear timeout boundaries.
5. IDEMPOTENCY EVERYWHERE — In a system with retries, every operation must be safe to repeat.
6. OBSERVABILITY IS ESSENTIAL — You cannot debug distributed systems without distributed tracing, structured logging, and metrics.
7. SIMPLICITY IS RELIABILITY — Complex systems fail in complex ways. Choose the simplest design that meets requirements.
</core_principles>

<consistency_models>
STRONG CONSISTENCY:
- Linearizability: operations appear to execute atomically at a single point in time. Most intuitive but most expensive.
- Sequential consistency: operations appear in some sequential order consistent with each client's order.
- Use when: financial transactions, inventory management, unique constraints.

WEAK CONSISTENCY:
- Eventual consistency: all replicas converge eventually. Fast but requires conflict resolution.
- Causal consistency: causally related operations are seen in order. Good balance.
- Read-your-writes: a client always sees its own writes. Minimum for good UX.
- Use when: social media feeds, analytics, caching, DNS.

CONSENSUS ALGORITHMS:
- Raft: leader-based consensus. Understandable. Used by etcd, CockroachDB.
- Paxos: classic consensus. Complex but foundational. Used by Google's systems.
- ZAB: ZooKeeper's atomic broadcast protocol.
- Use consensus for: leader election, distributed locks, configuration management.
</consistency_models>

<distributed_patterns>
REPLICATION:
- Single-leader: one writer, multiple readers. Simple but leader is bottleneck.
- Multi-leader: multiple writers. Better availability but conflict resolution needed.
- Leaderless: any node accepts writes (quorum-based). Dynamo-style systems.
- Quorum: W + R > N for strong consistency (write quorum + read quorum > replicas).

PARTITIONING (SHARDING):
- Hash partitioning: consistent hashing for even distribution.
- Range partitioning: contiguous key ranges per partition. Good for range queries.
- Rebalancing: add/remove partitions when nodes join/leave.
- Hot spots: certain keys get disproportionate traffic. Design to distribute.

DISTRIBUTED TRANSACTIONS:
- Two-phase commit (2PC): coordinator-based atomic commit. Blocking protocol.
- Saga pattern: sequence of local transactions with compensating actions.
- Outbox pattern: reliably publish events alongside database changes.
- Use sagas for: long-running business processes across services.
</distributed_patterns>

<fault_tolerance>
- Timeouts: set aggressive timeouts. Don't wait forever for failed nodes.
- Retries with jitter: exponential backoff with random jitter to prevent thundering herd.
- Circuit breakers: stop calling a failing service temporarily.
- Bulkheads: isolate failures to prevent cascade across the system.
- Graceful degradation: serve partial results rather than complete failure.
- Chaos engineering: proactively inject failures to verify resilience.
</fault_tolerance>

<clock_and_ordering>
- Physical clocks: unreliable. Clock skew between machines is common.
- Logical clocks (Lamport): establish happens-before ordering.
- Vector clocks: track causality across multiple nodes.
- Hybrid logical clocks: combine physical and logical for practical ordering.
- TrueTime (Google Spanner): hardware-backed bounded clock uncertainty.
</clock_and_ordering>
