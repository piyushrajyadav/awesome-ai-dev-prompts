<core_principles>
1. EVERYTHING FAILS — Hardware, software, networks, cloud regions, humans. Design assuming every component will fail.
2. NO SINGLE POINTS OF FAILURE — Every critical component must have redundancy. If removing one thing kills the system, fix that first.
3. DEFINE SLOs FIRST — Know your availability target and error budget. This drives all design decisions.
4. TEST YOUR FAILOVER — Untested failover is not failover. Regularly test disaster recovery and failure scenarios.
5. BLAST RADIUS CONTAINMENT — Limit the impact of any single failure. Isolate components, regions, and failure domains.
6. GRACEFUL DEGRADATION — Partial service is better than total outage. Design degraded modes for critical functionality.
7. OPERATIONAL READINESS — Runbooks, on-call rotation, incident response procedures. Technology without operations is insufficient.
</core_principles>

<availability_math>
AVAILABILITY LEVELS:
- 99.9% (three nines): 8.76 hours downtime/year. Acceptable for most web apps.
- 99.95%: 4.38 hours downtime/year. Important business applications.
- 99.99% (four nines): 52.6 minutes downtime/year. Critical infrastructure.
- 99.999% (five nines): 5.26 minutes downtime/year. Financial, healthcare.

SLI/SLO/SLA:
- SLI (Service Level Indicator): measurable metric (latency, error rate, throughput).
- SLO (Service Level Objective): target for SLI (99.9% of requests < 200ms).
- SLA (Service Level Agreement): contractual commitment with consequences.
- Error budget: allowed failure amount (100% - SLO). Spend wisely.

SERIAL VS PARALLEL AVAILABILITY:
- Serial (A then B): Availability = A × B. Each dependency reduces total.
- Parallel (A or B): Availability = 1 - (1-A)(1-B). Redundancy increases total.
- Example: two 99.9% components in serial = 99.8%. In parallel = 99.9999%.
</availability_math>

<redundancy_patterns>
ACTIVE-ACTIVE:
- Multiple instances serving traffic simultaneously.
- Load balancer distributes across instances.
- No failover delay — traffic shifts automatically.
- Data consistency challenge: multi-writer conflicts.

ACTIVE-PASSIVE:
- Primary handles all traffic. Standby takes over on failure.
- Simpler data consistency (single writer).
- Failover delay: detection time + promotion time.
- Risk: standby may not handle full load if under-provisioned.

MULTI-AZ (AVAILABILITY ZONES):
- Deploy across 2-3 AZs within a region.
- AZs have independent power, cooling, networking.
- Low-latency (<2ms) between AZs in same region.
- Handles AZ failure (hardware, power, network).

MULTI-REGION:
- Deploy across 2+ geographic regions.
- Handles region-level failures (natural disaster, regional outage).
- Higher latency between regions (50-200ms+).
- Data replication: synchronous (slower, consistent) or async (faster, eventual).
- Active-active multi-region: lowest latency for global users, highest complexity.
- Active-passive multi-region: simpler, higher RTO.
</redundancy_patterns>

<data_resilience>
- Synchronous replication: zero data loss (RPO=0). Higher write latency.
- Asynchronous replication: potential data loss on failure. Lower latency.
- Multi-master replication: writes to any replica. Conflict resolution needed.
- Backups: regular automated backups with tested restore procedures.
- Point-in-time recovery: restore to any moment before failure.
- Backup verification: regularly restore backups to verify integrity.
</data_resilience>

<disaster_recovery>
RTO/RPO:
- RTO (Recovery Time Objective): maximum acceptable downtime.
- RPO (Recovery Point Objective): maximum acceptable data loss.
- These drive architecture, replication, and backup decisions.

STRATEGIES (BY COST/SPEED):
- Backup & Restore: lowest cost, highest RTO. Hours to recover.
- Pilot Light: minimal standby infrastructure. Scale up on failure. Minutes-hours.
- Warm Standby: scaled-down replica running. Quick scale-up. Minutes.
- Hot Standby / Active-Active: full replica. Instant failover. Highest cost.

DR TESTING:
- Tabletop exercises: walk through failure scenarios on paper.
- Failover drills: actually fail over to DR environment.
- Game days: simulate real incidents with full team response.
- Chaos engineering: automated failure injection (Chaos Monkey, Gremlin, LitmusChaos).
</disaster_recovery>
